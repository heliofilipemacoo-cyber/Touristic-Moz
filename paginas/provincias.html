Introduction & First Considerations

Mozambique is divided into 10 provinces plus Maputo City, which holds provincial status of its own. To keep this guide genuinely useful for travelers rather than an exhaustive administrative list, we focus only on the provinces that offer real value for a typical trip: places with established tourist infrastructure, notable attractions, and reasonably easy access.

Below you will find in-depth coverage of Maputo (capital and gateway to the country), Inhambane (beaches and diving), Nampula (home to the UNESCO-listed Ilha de Moçambique), Sofala (Gorongosa National Park and wildlife), and Cabo Delgado (the Quirimbas Archipelago).

The remaining provinces — Niassa, Zambézia, Tete, Manica, and Gaza — are not covered in detail here. This isn't because they lack value; it's simply that they see far fewer international visitors, have less tourist infrastructure in place, and are generally harder to reach without a longer, more independent trip. If your travel style leans toward off-the-beaten-path exploration, they're worth researching separately.

⚠ Safety Notice: Cabo Delgado is currently subject to "avoid all travel" advisories from several governments (including the US, UK, Canada and Australia) due to an active insurgency in the province since 2017. Before planning any visit, check your government's official travel advisory for Mozambique.

Cabo Delegado

Historically, Cabo Delgado has been home to the Quirimbas Archipelago — a chain of pristine coral islands considered one of Mozambique's most exceptional destinations.

The Quirimbas Archipelago

Stretching for over 400 km along the coast, the Quirimbas Archipelago is made up of around 32 coral islands, most of them uninhabited and untouched by mass tourism. Crystal-clear turquoise waters, coral reefs teeming with marine life, and powder-white sandbanks make it one of the most exclusive island escapes in the Indian Ocean.

Ibo Island is the archipelago's cultural heart — a former Swahili-Arab and Portuguese trading post, with crumbling colonial forts, silver filigree workshops, and a slow, timeless pace that draws travelers looking for something beyond the typical beach holiday.

Further out, islands like Vamizi, Medjumbe, and Matemo are home to a handful of ultra-exclusive private lodges, popular for diving and snorkeling among some of the healthiest coral reefs in the region, as well as big-game fishing and traditional dhow sailing trips between islands.

Wildlife lovers can also spot humpback whales during migration season (roughly June to September), dolphins, and nesting sea turtles on several of the islands' beaches.

As beautiful as the Quirimbas Archipelago is, please revisit the safety notice above — the security situation in Cabo Delgado should be your starting point when deciding whether and how to travel here, not an afterthought.

A Bit of History

Long before European colonization, Cabo Delgado's coast — particularly Ibo Island, part of the Quirimbas — was a significant Swahili-Arab trading post, connecting East Africa to the Indian Ocean trade routes. Ibo still holds colonial-era fort ruins and a distinct architectural character shaped by that history.

In more recent years, the discovery of major offshore natural gas reserves has drawn significant international investment to the province, reshaping its economy alongside the security challenges mentioned above.

Other Highlights

Pemba, the provincial capital, sits on one of the largest natural bays in the world. Wimbi Beach, its main stretch of coastline, was once a popular base for travelers heading further into the Quirimbas — with palm-lined sand, dhow trips, and a relaxed, low-key atmosphere.

Mecúfi, a short drive south of Pemba, is known for dramatic red sandstone cliffs overlooking the ocean — a striking, less-visited stretch of coast compared to the archipelago's more famous islands.

Inland, the province borders the Rovuma River, which forms the natural frontier with Tanzania — an area of mangroves, fishing communities, and river life largely untouched by tourism infrastructure.
